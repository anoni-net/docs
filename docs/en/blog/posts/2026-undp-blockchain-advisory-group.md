Say "blockchain" and most people picture token prices, meme coins, or the next exchange to blow up. The UN Development Programme (UNDP) is working from a different script. On 3 June 2026, during Proof of Talk in Paris, it convened 26 blockchain foundations and ecosystems — Ethereum, Cardano, Stellar, Avalanche, and more — into a new **Blockchain Advisory Group (BAG)**, to explore how the technology might serve digital identity, financial inclusion, and public governance.

For a community that spends its days on Tor, Tails, and OONI, the interesting part is not the guest list. It is that "blockchain for public good" lands squarely on the questions we care about most: once identity and payments move on-chain, who can see your records, and who gets to decide how they are used.

<!-- more -->

## What BAG actually is

BAG is an advisory body, not a deployment arm. It is chaired by UNDP Associate Administrator Haoliang Xu, meets twice a year, and opened with **financial inclusion and digital finance** as its first deep-dive theme. Its stated priority areas span public trust and digital governance; inclusive society and legal identity; financial inclusion and digital financial services; sustainability, climate, and trackable responsibility; and digital labour and the future of work.

The 26 members are worth a glance for their composition rather than their names. Alongside the major chain foundations sit public-goods outfits like the Blockchain for Good Alliance, Funding the Commons, and Giveth, plus one exchange (Kraken) and a media name (Cointelegraph). This is a room of foundations and research bodies more than token issuers. UNDP's innovation initiative **AltFinLab** ran a "Scaling Blockchain for Public Good" event series alongside the launch.

One term recurs in all of this: **DPI**, or digital public infrastructure — the digital-age equivalent of roads, water, and the power grid, usually meaning three layers: identity (how you prove who you are), payments (how money moves), and data exchange (how systems talk to each other). UNDP treats blockchain as one possible way to strengthen DPI, and most of BAG's agenda lives inside that frame.

## The problem UNDP is reaching for

The most telling theme is **legal identity**. The World Bank's [ID4D](https://www.worldbank.org/en/programs/id4d){target="_blank"} program tracks the hundreds of millions of people worldwide who lack official proof of identity. Without it, you are shut out of opening a bank account, enrolling in school, collecting benefits, or receiving cross-border aid. Financial inclusion is the same problem in another form: fragmented payment rails, expensive remittances, and people locked out of the formal financial system.

UNDP is not starting from zero. It has run field experiments across Africa, Europe, Latin America, and Asia-Pacific, spanning digital payments, climate finance, and identity-and-eligibility verification. A few concrete cases:

- **Bosnia and Herzegovina**: verifiable digital university diplomas, stored in an eIDAS-compliant digital wallet so credentials can be checked across borders.
- **Malawi**: a blockchain-backed system to register and verify households needing humanitarian aid, reducing duplicate or fraudulent claims.
- **The UN itself**: a "Digital Certificate of Entitlement" that modernized pension verification for more than 70,000 beneficiaries.

The common thread is checking identity and eligibility. And the moment identity and money become verifiable, traceable digital records, privacy questions arrive with them. That is where our reading begins.

## A privacy reading: three questions

**1. Blockchain does not equal privacy.** Most public chains are open ledgers; anyone can follow a transaction from end to end. Putting payments or identity on-chain can make money trails *easier* to trace, not harder. So "blockchain for public good" deserves a follow-up: is this a public chain, a privacy-preserving design like Monero or Zcash, or zero-knowledge proofs (a method to prove a statement is true without revealing the underlying data)? The choice decides how visible your records are.

**2. Digital identity is fiercely contested.** Without protective design, digital-ID systems can exclude marginalized people, accrete into mass-surveillance databases, and leak sensitive data that cannot be un-leaked. From China's national internet ID (live since July 2025) to biometric ID pushes elsewhere, civil society keeps pushing back. In the UK, [millions signed a petition](https://petition.parliament.uk/petitions/730194){target="_blank"} against mandatory digital ID, with the Open Rights Group, Amnesty International, and Big Brother Watch all opposing. BAG's "legal identity" and DPI themes sit right in the middle of that debate.

**3. Governance, not technology, decides the outcome.** What really determines whether a digital ID helps or harms is the governance detail: whether data is minimized, whether you can opt out, who holds access, and whether there is redress when something breaks. Give someone with no ID a usable one and you solve real problems. Make the same system mandatory, centralized, and reverse-searchable, and it becomes a surveillance tool. The technology does not settle which way it goes; the governance does.

## A note from where we sit

anoni.net writes from Taiwan, and Taiwan is no stranger to this tension. Its New eID (digital identity card) was [halted in 2021](https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2021/01/22/2003751023){target="_blank"} over privacy and security concerns and the absence of a dedicated law. If a jurisdiction with relatively mature data-protection institutions stalled on exactly these questions — how the data is protected, whether it is mandatory, whether there is a law to govern it — those questions only get harder as UNDP scales digital identity into places where the legal scaffolding is thinner.

None of this makes BAG a bad idea. A usable identity is a real good for the people who lack one. It is a reminder that "for public good" is a claim about governance, not a property of the technology, and that is the lens we will keep applying as the group's work unfolds.
